Most job seekers have heard the term "ATS score" — but few understand what it actually measures or why it matters so much. This guide explains exactly how ATS scoring works and what you can do about it.

## What is an ATS?

An Applicant Tracking System (ATS) is software used by companies to manage job applications. When you submit a resume online, it almost always goes through an ATS before a human sees it.

The ATS does three things:
- **Parses** your resume into structured data (name, contact, experience, skills, education)
- **Ranks** it against the job description using an algorithm
- **Filters** out resumes below a certain threshold

According to research from Harvard Business Review, **75% of resumes are rejected by ATS before a human reviewer sees them**. Your resume could be perfect for a role and still get filtered out because of formatting or missing keywords.

## What does an ATS score measure?

Different ATS platforms calculate scores differently, but most evaluate the same core factors:

### 1. Keyword Match (highest weight)
The system compares the words in your resume against the words in the job description. Exact matches score highest. Some advanced systems also match synonyms — but most don't.

**Example:** If the JD says "machine learning" and your resume says "ML," basic ATS systems may not connect them. ResumeATS does.

### 2. Skills Alignment
How well does your skills section match the required and preferred skills in the posting? Both the presence of keywords and their placement (in a skills section vs. buried in a bullet) affects this.

### 3. Experience Relevance
Does your experience align with the role? ATS systems look for job titles, years of experience signals, industry terms, and action-oriented content.

### 4. Education Match
Does your education level and field match what's required? Some systems hard-filter on degree requirements.

### 5. ATS Compatibility
Can the system even read your resume? Resumes with tables, text boxes, columns, or image-based content often parse incorrectly — which tanks your score regardless of the content.

### 6. Formatting & Structure
Well-structured resumes with clear section headers (Experience, Skills, Education) score higher than creative layouts that confuse parsers.

## What's a good ATS score?

Scores vary by platform, but here's a practical benchmark:

| Score Range | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 85–100 | Top tier — highly likely to pass initial screening |
| 70–84 | Strong — competitive, minor tweaks recommended |
| 55–69 | Average — needs improvement to stand out |
| 40–54 | Below average — significant gaps in keyword alignment |
| Under 40 | Critical — likely being filtered out automatically |

Most career coaches recommend targeting **70+** before applying to any specific role.

## Why do most resumes score poorly?

The three most common reasons:

**1. Generic resumes sent to specific roles.** A resume you wrote for a "software engineer" role will score poorly against a "backend engineer — Python/Django" posting. Every application needs at least minor tailoring.

**2. Formatting that breaks parsers.** Canva templates, two-column layouts, headers with contact info, and text boxes all cause parsing failures. The ATS may extract garbled text — or nothing at all.

**3. Missing keywords that feel obvious.** Candidates often assume their experience speaks for itself. It doesn't — not to a machine. If the JD says "REST APIs" 8 times and your resume never says it, you'll lose to a less experienced candidate who did.

## How to improve your ATS score

### Step 1: Analyze before you apply
Use a tool like [ResumeATS](/analyze) to score your resume against the specific job description *before* you submit. This takes 5 minutes and dramatically improves your odds.

### Step 2: Add the exact keywords from the JD
Read the job posting. Every technology, skill, certification, and methodology mentioned is a potential keyword. Add the ones that apply to your experience — in context, not just as a list.

### Step 3: Fix your formatting
Use a clean, single-column layout. Standard section headers (Experience, Skills, Education). No tables. No text boxes. Saved as DOCX or text-based PDF.

### Step 4: Quantify your achievements
Numbers signal impact. "Reduced API latency by 40%" beats "improved performance." Numbers also create unique context that differentiates your resume.

### Step 5: Tailor your summary
The professional summary is the first thing both ATS and recruiters read. Spend 10 minutes writing a 2–3 sentence summary that mirrors the language and priorities in the JD.
